# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two equally weighted groups, one shared context:

- **CS students** learning Git as part of coursework — lab sessions and evening study, often anxious about "breaking the repo".
- **Self-taught beginners** (bootcampers, hobbyists) learning on their own time, motivated by visible progression and rewards.

Both arrive in focused solo sessions, usually on a laptop in low ambient light, with a concrete job: practice a Git scenario until the repository state comes out right. The workspace (terminal + live DAG) is where they spend most of their time; the hub, level-map, and quest surfaces are where they orient, choose, and feel progress.

## Product Purpose

GIT it! is a scenario-driven Git learning platform. It replaces command memorization with practical problem-solving: a simulated terminal, a live animated repository DAG, and state-based evaluation in a consequence-safe environment. Scaffolding fades from guided (Easy) to independent (Hard); retries use structurally changed variants so learning transfers.

Success looks like: a learner who reasons about repository state instead of pasting commands, returns voluntarily because progress feels earned, and clears the Easy → Medium → Hard chain per skill focus.

## Brand Personality

**Arcane, immersive, premium.** A medieval-fantasy world rendered in neon — the level map, quests, encounters, and GitCoin economy are a real game layer, not decoration. Atmosphere is identity: living skies, glow, depth, 3D eye-candy. But the register is product: inside the practice workspace, immersion yields to focus — the terminal and DAG stay distraction-free, and the fantasy frames the work rather than interrupting it.

Voice: a confident guide in the world, not a textbook. Short, concrete, in-fiction where it helps ("quest", "storey", "encounter"), plain Git vocabulary where precision matters.

## Anti-references

- **Flat card grids and generic SaaS dashboards.** Rows of identical stat cards, icon + heading + paragraph tiles. If a surface could be mistaken for an admin template, it has failed.
- **Wordy explainer UI.** Long paragraphs of instruction, tooltip essays, walls of helper text. Show state, don't narrate it.
- **Cutesy edu-gamification (Duolingo-style).** Mascot cheerfulness, confetti-for-everything, streak guilt. Rewards here feel earned and premium, not sugary.
- **Random decoration.** Every visual flourish must be deliberate and placed; scattered particles/glows with no compositional intent read as noise.

## Design Principles

1. **The world is the interface.** Navigation and progress are expressed through the game world (chapter nodes, quest doors, chests) rather than abstract menus — spatial and physical over list-and-label.
2. **Immersion outside, clarity inside.** Hub/level-map/quest surfaces carry the atmosphere; the practice workspace is ruthlessly legible — terminal, DAG, and feedback win every contest with decoration.
3. **Progress must feel earned.** Rewards (coins, unlocks, mastery) are paced and weighty. No free dopamine; celebration scales with achievement.
4. **State over story.** The repository DAG is the ground truth of every interaction. When in doubt, make the state more visible, not the explanation longer.
5. **Deliberate eye-candy.** Depth, glow, and motion are part of the build, never an afterthought — each effect has a reason, a place, and a reduced-motion fallback.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal compliance target. Practical defaults still apply: readable contrast for body text on the dark neon theme (glow colors reserved for accents, not prose), `prefers-reduced-motion` alternatives for the motion-heavy surfaces (living sky, DAG animation), and pass/fail states that don't rely on color alone.
